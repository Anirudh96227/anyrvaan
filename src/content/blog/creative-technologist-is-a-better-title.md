"Designer" quietly implies someone else builds it. "Developer" quietly implies someone else decided what it should be. Both titles assume a handoff — a moment where the thing leaves one person's hands and lands in another's. I've started to think that handoff is exactly where a lot of the interesting work now falls through the cracks.

"Creative technologist" gets mocked sometimes for sounding like a title assembled to dodge a decision. I don't see it that way anymore. It feels more like a title that refuses to pretend the two lanes are still separate — because for a growing amount of real work, they just aren't.

## The lanes stopped being separate

Take the background animation on this site's homepage. You can't really judge whether it's well designed without understanding why it costs nothing when nobody's touching it — that restraint *is* the design. And you can't build it well without knowing what it's supposed to feel like when a cursor drifts close. The design decision and the engineering decision turned out to be the same decision, just wearing two different hats.

Split that across two people and two handoffs, and in my experience you reliably get one of two failures: something beautiful and slow, or something fast and forgettable. The one idea that made it both beautiful and nearly free had to live in one head the whole time. (The full build is in [Building a Cursor-Reactive Particle Field](/blog/building-a-cursor-reactive-particle-field/).)

## Three titles, honestly compared

<div class="table-wrap">

| | Designer | Developer | Creative technologist |
|---|---|---|---|
| Owns | What it should be | How it's built | The whole distance between the two |
| Quietly assumes | Someone else implements it | Someone else specified it | No one is handing off |
| Common blind spot | Ideas that can't be built well | Building the wrong thing beautifully | Spreading too thin to master either edge |
| Best when | The problem is mostly taste | The problem is mostly execution | The idea and the build are the same problem |

</div>

I'm not arguing everyone should work solo, or that specialists stopped mattering — they didn't, and deep single-discipline mastery is still its own rare thing. What I mean is narrower: the title should describe what you're actually good at, and "carries an idea into implementation without losing it in translation" doesn't fit cleanly under either half alone.

## The real test is the seam, not the résumé

Here's how I'd tell if this title genuinely describes someone, rather than being two skills stapled together on a CV. It's not "can you design and code." Plenty of people can do both adequately and are still, correctly, one or the other.

The test is whether you can feel the seam. Can you tell, unprompted, when a design decision is secretly a performance problem? When a technical constraint is secretly a design opportunity hiding in plain sight? That instinct — living in the gap between the two disciplines — is the thing single-discipline titles never think to look for, because it's nobody's job to look there. If that's where you're most useful, you probably already are one of these, whatever your business card says.
