You start your creative life in a house you didn't build. It's made of whatever tools happened to be available, the conventions of whatever field you fell into, the taste you absorbed before you had opinions of your own, the defaults nobody chose but everyone just follows. For a long time I mistook this house for reality itself — it was just "how things are done." I think the whole arc of getting good is realizing it's a house, made of choices, and that you're allowed to go build a different one.

## Inherited defaults are invisible until you name them

The tricky part is that the house you're born into never announces itself. Nobody tells you "left-aligned lists are a convention, not a law," or "this is just the default your software happened to ship with." These things arrive disguised as the natural order of things. And you can't choose differently about something you can't even see as a choice yet.

<div class="table-wrap">

| The house you're born into | The house you build |
|---|---|
| Inherited, absorbed, unexamined | Chosen, deliberate, defended |
| "That's just how it's done" | "I do it this way, for this reason" |
| Invisible until named | Visible because you built it |
| Whatever the tools defaulted to | Whatever the work actually needs |
| Feels like reality | Known to be a choice |

</div>

## Building on purpose

Naming an inherited default doesn't automatically mean rejecting it. Plenty of conventions exist for good reasons, and the point was never contrarianism for its own sake. The point is agency — moving each thing from the first column to the second, so the conventions you keep are ones you've actually chosen, and the ones you drop are dropped on purpose. A house you build can still hold plenty of inherited furniture. The difference is that now you're the one who put it there.

This is part of why the studio treats its own foundations — the type system, the approach to motion, the whole restraint — as decisions worth writing down, rather than defaults worth assuming without a second look. (Those write-ups, like [Notes on an Editorial Type System](/blog/notes-on-an-editorial-type-system/), exist partly to make the choices visible even to me.) A practice you can't articulate is still just a house you're living in. A practice you can explain is one you actually built.

<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>List three things about how you work that you've never questioned. Those are walls of the house you were born into — start there.</span></li>
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>For each, ask: is this a real reason, or just a default I absorbed? Be honest about how many are simply "the software came like this."</span></li>
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>Keep the conventions that survive the question. But now you're keeping them on purpose, which is a different thing than never having asked.</span></li>
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>Change one inherited default deliberately, just to feel that you can. Agency is a muscle, and it needs using now and then.</span></li>
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>Write down the choices that define your practice. If you can explain the house, you built it. If you can't, you're probably still just living in it.</span></li>
</ul>
