There are eight finished pieces in [*Rough Cut Divinity*](/work/rough-cut-divinity/). What's not on that page is the real count of generations behind them — a lot closer to a couple hundred than to eight. I used to think that ratio was a confession of inefficiency. I don't anymore. It's just the actual shape of the work now, and almost nobody talks about it honestly.

The popular story of AI-assisted creation is: describe it, get it, ship it. What actually happens, at least for me, is: describe it, get it wrong nineteen times in ways I can't immediately articulate, and slowly work out — by sitting with the failures — what I meant in the first place.

## Generating was the bottleneck. Now editing is.

For most of history, the constraint on making things was making them. A painter with a clear idea still had to spend hours executing it. That's flipped. Execution is instant and nearly free now; what's scarce is knowing which of the instant results is right, and being able to say why.

That's a genuinely different skill, worth naming because it's the one you get paid for now. It isn't "prompting better." It's editing — the unglamorous work of looking at twenty plausible options and rejecting nineteen for reasons specific enough to actually act on.

## The specific enemy is "generic correctness"

The failure mode you'll hit most isn't ugliness. It's competence with no point of view. Ask for "a bolt of lightning becoming a sword" and you'll reliably get something correct, atmospheric, and completely forgettable — because it's the statistical average of every version of that image the model has seen.

<div class="table-wrap">

| The obvious answer (what you get first) | The keeper (what you're actually after) |
|---|---|
| A finished sword, mid-swing | Lightning that hasn't decided to be a sword yet |
| A god fully formed | Two forms mid-merge, neither one finished |
| A lotus in full bloom | A lotus still opening, lit from somewhere inside |
| "Correct," average, instantly forgettable | Specific, deliberate, unmistakably one idea |

</div>

You don't reach the right-hand column by asking the model to try harder. I've found you reach it by getting bored of the average enough times that it runs out of average things to show you. The rejection is the creative act — maybe the only one that's really yours in this process.

## Why the ratio isn't waste

Twenty-to-one sounds wasteful until you realize the nineteen rejects are how you find out what you actually mean. Each one you turn down for a specific reason — "the light has no logic here," "this has already resolved, I need it a beat earlier" — sharpens the target a little. Skip that step and you're regenerating on vibes, hoping the fifth try beats the first without ever learning why one did. (This is the hands-on half of a bigger point I made in [AI Made Creation Cheap. Taste Got Expensive.](/blog/ai-made-creation-cheap-taste-got-expensive/))

So maybe the number worth being proud of isn't how few you generated. It's how precisely you can explain the ones you killed.

## An editing practice

<ul class="checklist">
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>On your next AI-assisted piece, deliberately generate more than feels comfortable before choosing. You can't edit a range you haven't seen.</span></li>
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>Reject out loud. For each option you pass on, say the one specific reason. If the reason is "dunno, worse," you haven't found your standard yet.</span></li>
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>When something feels correct but boring, name that explicitly. It's the generic-average trap, and it's the single most common thing worth pushing past.</span></li>
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>Write the least obvious correct version of your idea before you generate anything. Aim at that, not at the first thing the prompt hands back.</span></li>
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>Keep a rejects folder for a month. Reviewing what you consistently threw out is the clearest mirror of your own taste you'll get.</span></li>
</ul>
