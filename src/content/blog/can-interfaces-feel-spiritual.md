Let me define the word carefully, because it's easy to roll your eyes at. I don't mean religious, and I definitely don't mean an app that quotes scripture at you. I mean the ordinary, secular experience of presence — being fully here, attention undivided, with a small sense of awe or stillness. A forest clearing can give you that. A great room can. What I kept wondering was whether an interface — a thing made of buttons and text — ever could, and if so, what that would actually take.

## What actually produces presence

The experiences that give people presence tend to share a few properties, and none of them are mystical. They give you space. They don't rush you. They reward attention instead of fracturing it. They have a stillness that makes you slow down to match it. Software almost never has these properties — most interfaces are built to do the opposite, fill every pixel, fracture attention into badges and notifications. But the properties themselves are entirely achievable in software. They're just rarely chosen.

<div class="table-wrap">

| Interfaces that agitate | Interfaces that create presence |
|---|---|
| Fill every pixel | Leave deliberate space |
| Rush you toward the next tap | Let you set the pace |
| Fracture attention with badges | Reward sustained attention |
| Busy, always moving | Still, moving only with reason |
| You leave slightly depleted | You leave slightly restored |

</div>

## Restraint is the whole technique

There's no trick beyond restraint, honestly. Presence in an interface comes from what you don't put there — the notification you don't send, the animation you don't add, the empty space you don't fill in. That's an uncomfortable thing to design toward, because every instinct and every metric pushes you toward more, and "I left this deliberately empty" is a hard sentence to defend in a review. But that emptiness is exactly where presence lives. A room feels sacred partly because it isn't crammed. Maybe a screen can too.

This is close to what a subtractive spiritual practice teaches about the self — that what remains after you stop adding is more real than the pile you spent years assembling. I wrote about borrowing that idea for design in [What Advaita Taught Me About Deleting Things](/blog/what-advaita-taught-me-about-deleting-things/). I'd stop short of calling any interface holy. But one that gives you space, respects your attention, and asks you to slow down can genuinely produce a quiet, secular version of presence. And that's rare enough to be worth designing for on purpose.

<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>Find the busiest screen in your product and remove one thing that fractures attention. Sit with the extra space instead of filling it back in.</span></li>
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>Ask whether your interface rewards sustained attention or punishes it. Count the interruptions — each one is presence leaking out.</span></li>
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>Let the user set the pace somewhere it currently rushes them. Presence and hurry don't really coexist.</span></li>
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>Practice defending emptiness out loud. "I left this space on purpose" is a real decision, not an unfinished one.</span></li>
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>After using your own product, notice how you feel — depleted or restored. That feeling is a design outcome you're responsible for, whether you meant it or not.</span></li>
</ul>
