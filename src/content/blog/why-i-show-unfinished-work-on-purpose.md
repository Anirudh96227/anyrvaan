There's a strong instinct to only show finished work — the clean final, the hero shot, the thing with all the seams sanded off. It feels safer. But it quietly reinforces a myth: that good work arrives fully formed, and that the mess in *your* process means you're doing it wrong. Showing unfinished work on purpose breaks that myth, and it turns out to be the more useful and more persuasive move — not the riskier one.

## The finished piece hides the actual work

A polished final tells you *what* was made. It tells you almost nothing about *how*, or *why this and not the twenty other versions*. And the how and why are where the real expertise lives. When you only show finals, you're withholding the most valuable part and keeping only the part anyone can screenshot.

<div class="table-wrap">

| Showing only finished work | Showing the process too |
|---|---|
| Implies work arrives fully formed | Shows work is made, not summoned |
| Hides the reasoning | Reveals the judgment calls |
| Impressive but opaque | Impressive *and* teachable |
| "How did they do that?" | "Ah — that's how they did that" |
| Builds distance | Builds trust |

</div>

## Unfinished work is more convincing, not less

This is the counterintuitive part. You'd think the flawless final is more persuasive, but a visible process is often what actually earns trust — because it's evidence of judgment, not just luck. Anyone can get one good result by accident. Showing the rejected directions, the reasoning, the iterations proves the good result was *chosen*, which is a far stronger claim than "look what appeared." The studio keeps an [Experiments](/experiments) section for exactly this reason: unfinished on purpose, because the in-progress state is where the thinking is legible.

There's a discipline to it, though. "Showing unfinished work" doesn't mean dumping every scrap — that's just noise. It means showing the *instructive* parts: the fork in the road, the thing you tried that failed and why, the decision that turned the piece. Curate the mess for what it teaches, and it stops being exposure and becomes generosity.

## How to Proceed

<ul class="checklist">
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>On your next finished piece, publish one artifact from the middle of the process too — a rejected direction, an early sketch, a note.</span></li>
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>For that artifact, write the one sentence that makes it instructive: what it shows about a decision. Curate the mess, don't just dump it.</span></li>
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>Notice the fear that comes up. That fear is the myth — that showing process makes you look less capable. It does the opposite.</span></li>
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>Show at least one thing you tried that failed, and why. A visible dead end proves the final was a choice, not an accident.</span></li>
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>Keep a space — even a private folder — for work-in-progress you're willing to show. You can't share process you didn't keep.</span></li>
</ul>
