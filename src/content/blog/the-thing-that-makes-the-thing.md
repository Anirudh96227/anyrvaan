The first video I made for this studio was one clip. The next was a set of eight. Somewhere around the third project I noticed I wasn't writing separate ideas anymore — I was writing one description, locking it, and changing exactly one thing per piece. That's the moment a project quietly stops being a thing you make and starts being a system that makes things. I still can't always tell it's happening while it's happening. This is about how I've learned to notice it after the fact, and what I do once I have.

## The tell: you're repeating a decision, not making one

Here's the signal I watch for now. If I catch myself re-deciding the same thing every time I start a new piece — the same color rules, the same layout logic, the same argument with myself about margins — that decision belongs in a system, not in my head.

On [*Rough Cut Divinity*](/work/rough-cut-divinity/), eight short pieces from two artists, the underlying brief for every single one followed the same four steps: direction, generation, selection, finishing. I didn't invent a new process eight times. I wrote it once, and used it eight times to look at a different subject. The creative work was designing that process carefully, once. Repeating it by hand each time wouldn't have made anything better — it would've just been eight more chances to quietly contradict myself.

## Why this raises your floor, not just your speed

The real payoff isn't doing less work, though that happens too. It's that the quality of everything you make afterward gets set by the system, not by whatever mood you're in that day. A tired version of me and a sharp version of me write roughly the same locked specification. They don't reliably invent equally good ideas from scratch, on demand, every single time.

<div class="table-wrap">

| | Making one thing | Building the thing that makes things |
|---|---|---|
| Where the effort goes | Into this specific output | Into the rules every output inherits |
| Quality depends on | How good you feel today | A decision you made once, carefully |
| Cost of the 10th piece | Same as the 1st | Near zero |
| Biggest risk | Inconsistency across pieces | Over-building before you know the rules |
| What you're really producing | An artifact | A standard |

</div>

This site runs the same way, one level up, if I'm honest about it. Every case study — a video, a gallery, an embedded clip — is a text file plus a shared set of rules, not a hand-built page. Adding a new project means writing content, not building a new layout. The portfolio isn't really the collection of finished pieces anymore. It's the small, unglamorous configuration that turns a folder of writing into a set of consistent pages.

## The trap: building the system too early

There's a failure mode on the other side, and I've fallen into it. If you build the machine before you've made anything by hand, you're systematizing guesses — you don't actually know yet which decisions are stable enough to lock. The honest sequence, as far as I can tell, is: make a few by hand, notice what you keep re-deciding, *then* extract that into a system. Systematize the pattern you've proven. Not the one you're hoping for.

## How to Proceed

<ul class="checklist">
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>Look at your last three projects. Write down every decision that came out the same way in all three. That list is your system, waiting to be extracted.</span></li>
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>Pick the single most-repeated decision and write it down once, explicitly, as a rule. Not in your head — on paper or in a file.</span></li>
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>On your next piece, change only one variable from the last one. Notice how much faster it goes when the rest is already locked.</span></li>
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>Resist systematizing anything you've done fewer than three times. If you can't point to the repetition, you're guessing.</span></li>
<li><span class="tick" aria-hidden="true"><svg viewBox="0 0 24 24"><path d="M5 12.5l4 4L19 6.5"/></svg></span><span>Once a month, ask what you're still doing by hand that you've now done enough times to trust. Promote it into the system.</span></li>
</ul>
