A couple of years ago, making something — a poster, a short video, a series of illustrations — took money, or a skill most people didn't have. Now it takes a sentence and a few minutes of waiting. I noticed the shift properly the day "I made this with AI" stopped being an interesting thing to say about a piece of work. Everyone reading this has the same generator I do.

So the question I keep coming back to isn't whether to use it. It's: once the tool is free and everyone has it, what's actually left to be good at? I don't have a tidy answer, but I do have a rubric I use on myself, and it's held up well enough that I wanted to write it down.

## The skill isn't prompting

Prompting has a ceiling, and you hit it in a few weekends. What doesn't have a ceiling — at least I haven't found one yet — is knowing which of the twenty options in front of you is the right one, and being able to say precisely *why* the other nineteen aren't.

That second part matters more than it sounds like it should. "I don't know, this one just feels better" isn't really a skill. You can't teach it to a client who's paying you to make the call. You can't improve it on purpose, because you can't tell what you're actually reacting to. What I've found works instead is naming the specific thing that's wrong. Once you can name it, you can go fix exactly that thing on the next attempt, instead of re-rolling the whole prompt and hoping.

I built that table the slow way — by getting it wrong, over and over, on [*Rough Cut Divinity*](/work/rough-cut-divinity/), eight short devotional and elemental pieces. Left alone, the generator will happily hand you lightning that's already a sword, a lotus already fully open, a merge already resolved into one clean god. Technically fine. Instantly forgettable, because it's the average of every version of that image the model has ever been shown. The keeper only showed up once I could say exactly what was wrong with the obvious version — not "try again," but "this has already finished becoming what it's about to become, and I need it a beat earlier."

## Why this is actually good news

If judgment is the scarce thing now, that's a better place to be than it sounds. Judgment is learnable in a way raw execution skill never quite was — you don't need ten years of drawing practice to get better at it, you need reps of rejecting things for reasons you can actually say out loud. Every generation you correctly diagnose is one of those reps. Most people doing this professionally right now aren't doing them. They're regenerating on vibes, hoping the fifth attempt beats the first, without ever working out why one did.

The tools got cheap. The judgment didn't. That gap is where the paid work lives now — and unlike prompting, it's a gap you can actually close on purpose.
