[Dashboard Cinema](/work/dashboard-cinema/) is a one-minute film where a screen full of numbers has an emergency and recovers, and nobody says a word. What I want to write down is how it was directed — because the choices weren't just visual taste. Each one is a trick borrowed from a film director who solved the same problem in movies, then applied to a wall of data.

## Hitchcock: let the audience know first

The most important one comes from Alfred Hitchcock. He drew a line between surprise and suspense. Surprise is a bomb going off with no warning — a second of shock, then it's over and forgotten. Suspense is showing you the bomb under the table early, and letting you sweat while the people at the table chat away, not knowing.

So in the corner of the screen, a small line starts creeping upward a few seconds before any alarm goes off, and well before the mouse pointer reacts. You see the trouble before anyone in the story does. That gap — you knowing, them not — is the whole first stretch of the film, and the quiet dread it creates does the job a voiceover would otherwise have to do. No caption could make you lean in the way just watching that line climb does.

## One slow move for the whole film

The next trick: don't cut. The whole minute is one single, slow camera move — easing gently inward as the trouble builds, snapping tight the moment the alarm fires, then drifting slowly back out as things recover. There's no editing, no jumping around. The camera isn't decoration sitting on top of the tension. The camera *is* the tension. When it finally settles back to where it began, you feel the breath out before you've read a single word telling you it's over.

## The trick that makes it honest

None of this would hold together if the screen were faking it, and the honesty comes down to one thing. Everything you see runs off a single measurement — one number rising and falling over the minute. Every other number, every chart, the color of the warning, the little status light: all of them are worked out from that one value. So nothing on screen can ever contradict anything else. When that line climbs, it's a real problem genuinely spreading, not an animation pretending to look tense for you.

The one thing I had to fight hardest: recovery has to be slower than the breakdown. A line that snaps straight back to healthy reads as fake, because real things don't heal instantly — they drag, and then they're better. Making the fix *feel* earned meant making the recovery gentler and slower than the collapse. The whole film, and its calm opposite, are [Dashboard Cinema](/work/dashboard-cinema/) and [a field guide to quiet apps](/work/anti-ui-atlas/).
