Every myth has a climax it loves to replay on command — Excalibur drawn, the deduction spoken aloud, the wings unfurled at the cliff's edge. This project asked a pettier, more interesting question: what was everyone doing on the one day none of that was required of them?

Seven miniature claymation portraits, art-directed frame by frame through prompt rather than sculpted by hand, each built around a figure whose entire cultural memory is one signature act — and each caught instead in the moment right beside it. Sherlock lets a mystery stay unsolved. Arthur puts the sword back. Daedalus finishes the wings and leaves them on the bench. Same seven-house village throughout, same warm unexplained light, same rule holding every plate together: no gloss, no spectacle, just one still gesture sitting at the center of the frame, doing nothing anyone will remember them for.

The final plate finally gathers all seven into one courtyard — the lens lowered, the book shut, the sword sheathed, the wings still folded on the ground where they were left. One evening, the whole village exhales at the same time.

Heroes are usually written as verbs — the deducing, the drawing, the flying. This is the version of them written as nouns instead: seven people, briefly, just being who they are when nothing's asking anything of them.
