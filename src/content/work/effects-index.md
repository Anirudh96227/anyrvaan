Here's the plain version of what this is. I make small animations with code instead of a camera — the kind you see on screens every day without thinking about them. A number ticking up to a million. A bar chart that fills as you watch. A loading spinner. Text that flies in and settles. Over about two years I made hundreds of these, one at a time.

And then I couldn't find them. That's the honest problem this whole thing came from. They were scattered across folders, named things I no longer remembered, and more than once I sat down and built one from scratch only to realize later I'd already made it months before. That's a rotten feeling — doing work you didn't need to do, because your own past work was lost to you.

So I did what a library does with books it can't afford to lose: I made an index. Ten short films, each one collecting animations that give you the same feeling — the ones that arrive, the ones that count and pulse, the ones that look like a screen full of live data. Sorting them by feeling instead of by date meant I could finally picture where something lived before I went looking for it.

The catch was simple and a little brutal: to fill ten films properly I needed far more animations than I had. There was no clever way around that, only honest choices, and I made two whole versions that didn't work before the third one did. That story — building the same thing three times and throwing the first two away — is its own post: [I built this three times before it worked](/blog/three-iterations-each-thrown-away/).

One more thing you'll notice watching them: they're calm. One shade of blue does all the pointing, everything else stays quiet, and nothing moves unless it has a reason to. That restraint is deliberate — when the frame holds still, the one thing that's animating is the only thing you look at, which is the whole point of a collection about animation. The same calm, slowed right down, became its quiet twin: [The Spiritual Series](/work/spiritual-series/).
