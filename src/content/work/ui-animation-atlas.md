This is a list — a careful, honest one — of a hundred small animations you see in apps every single day and mostly never think about. The little bounce when you pull a feed down to refresh it. The heart that bursts when you like a photo. The way a menu slides open, the way a loading placeholder shimmers. I wrote down all hundred of them, with two things attached to each: where it actually came from, and how it really works underneath.

It exists because I kept needing that information and couldn't trust my memory for it. When you're rebuilding one of these animations, "make it feel modern" is useless. You need the real thing — the exact speed, the exact bounce, the actual history. So instead of going by feel, I looked each one up and wrote down what's true.

And looking things up humbles you fast. Almost everyone remembers Twitter's little heart replacing the star — and a lot of them will tell you it happened in 2016. It was 2015, and it was built as a strip of 29 separate drawings played in sequence, which is exactly why a faithful copy has to flip through frames instead of smoothly growing. Get the year wrong and you'd probably build the wrong animation too. That kept happening: the confident memory was off, and the correction was the useful part.

The thing I'm proudest of is the honesty where I hit a wall. A couple of these, nobody can say for sure who first made them, or who first called that little pop-up notification a "toast." So I wrote down that we don't know, instead of picking a good-sounding answer. A reference is only worth anything if it admits where it's guessing — one made-up fact and you'd have to doubt all the rest. This is the collection that [Ten Apps, Rebuilt by Hand](/work/ui-replicas/) and [Retro Computing](/work/retro-computing/) draw from, and it has a mirror image pointed the other way: [a field guide to quiet apps](/work/anti-ui-atlas/), for apps that stay quiet instead of grabbing your attention.
