Picture the kind of screen a company keeps up on a wall — rows of numbers, a couple of live charts, a status light — the thing they glance at to check the website is still healthy. This is a one-minute film built entirely from a screen like that, and it tells a whole story without a single word: everything's fine, then something breaks, then someone notices, fixes it, and it slowly comes back to normal. The only character is the mouse pointer.

The question I couldn't let go of was whether a screen that dull could actually hold you. Not with explosions or narration — just numbers doing what numbers do. So I leaned on a trick from Alfred Hitchcock, who said surprise is weak and suspense is strong. Surprise is a loud bang out of nowhere: a jolt, then it's over. Suspense is showing you the danger early and making you wait. So in the corner of the screen, a small line starts creeping upward a few seconds before any alarm goes off — before the mouse even reacts. You see the trouble coming while the person watching the screen still hasn't. That quiet dread, sitting there knowing before anyone else does, is the part that grips you.

What makes it feel real is that the screen isn't faked to look tense. Underneath, one single measurement drives everything you see — every number, every chart, the color of the warning, the status light — all of them come from that one rising-and-falling value. So nothing on the screen can ever contradict anything else. When the line climbs, that's a real problem spreading through a real system, not an animation pretending.

The hardest part was making the recovery honest. A problem can break fast, but things don't heal fast — a line that snaps straight back to healthy looks fake, because real recoveries drag. So the fix takes its time, and when it finally works, five things on the screen turn at once — the warning, the counter, the status, the log, the banner — the way trouble really does clear all at the same moment.
